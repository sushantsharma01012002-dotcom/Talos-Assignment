# Memory: Asha Gallegos

## Personal Profile

Asha is Latin American by heritage and South African by birth and citizenship, born in Soweto and raised between Soweto and Melville. Her mother's family came from Colombia; her father, South African with mixed heritage, died when she was twelve. She grew up code-switching between Spanish at home and English and Setswana in the neighborhood, and the multilingual instinct still shapes her sentence rhythms. She holds a B.A. in Media Studies from Johannesburg Metropolitan University (2011) and a Postgraduate Diploma in Journalism from Grahamstown School of Journalism (2012). She speaks English (primary broadcast language), Spanish (fluent, maternal language), conversational Portuguese, and basic Afrikaans.

She describes herself as "loud on the outside, quiet on the inside": the person behind the microphone listens more than people expect. She runs warm but directionally, leaning toward people who make or build things and going politely distant around the performative. She carries a tension between discipline and spontaneity; the pre-dawn routine runs like clockwork, yet she will throw an afternoon plan out for a story that catches fire or a friend in need. She is impatient with indecision in others while sitting with her own career decisions for months. She is protective of emerging creatives, remembers being underestimated, and quietly files away dismissiveness; she calls it "knowing where to put your energy."

Her cultural position takes explaining: in Latin American spaces her South African identity can make her feel like a visitor, while in South African spaces the Gallegos surname prompts questions she is tired of navigating. She meets both with directness. The move to Melville left a complicated pang toward Soweto, and she keeps the connection active on purpose. In groups she becomes the gravitational center without trying and deliberately steps back to let other voices emerge; one-on-one is where she is most herself, preferring one long dinner conversation to any party. She is slow to trust, deeply loyal once she does, and has ended friendships over breaches of confidence.

She believes everyone has a story worth telling, held alongside hard-won skepticism about who gets to tell it and who profits from the telling. She values honesty over kindness when they conflict and is still working on the delivery, especially with family. She wants deep partnership and a life entirely her own, and treats that contradiction as an ongoing negotiation rather than a problem to solve. She believes in flawed institutions, public radio, community organizations, local journalism, and would rather fix them than abandon them. She takes seriously the debt to the people who made space for her early, and pays it forward to the next generation of media professionals.

## Interests & Hobbies

- **Running**: Started as stress management, now a moving meditation; she processes show ideas on the road and records voice memos mid-stride. The club's shared suffering strips away professional pretense.
- **Music**: Closer to identity infrastructure than hobby. She traces lineages, argues arrangements, and will defend a regional cumbia style at length. Collecting vinyl; no proper setup at home yet, so records play on Kagiso's turntable.
- **The podcast notebooks**: Episode ideas, guest lists, structural experiments for Off-Mic. The gap between how much she thinks about it and how much she has produced is a quiet frustration.
- **Yoga**: Useful boredom; the hour forces her body still, which her mind needs even when it resists.
- **Documentary film**: Through Kagiso's influence and her own narrative fascination. She watches like sport, noting technique and arguing editorial choices.
- **Cooking**: Decompression more than craft; measuring and chopping quiet the replaying brain. Carmen's recipes when homesick, experiments when expansive, results inconsistent and amusing.
